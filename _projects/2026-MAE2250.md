This project focused on developing a client-centered engineering design solution for reducing spotted lanternfly contamination during the grape harvesting process. Our team, LanternBye, designed a grape juice or sugar water-based density separation system that separates spotted lanternflies from harvested grapes before the grapes are sent for processing.

## Client Pitch

The Client Pitch introduced the problem of spotted lanternflies mixing with grapes during mechanical harvesting. Over the past decade, spotted lanternflies have become a significant issue for vineyards across the United States because they can negatively affect both grape quality and harvest quantity.

Our team chose to focus on the specific problem of spotted lanternflies being dislodged from vines along with grapes during the harvesting process. Since both grapes and lanternflies can enter the same collection stream after being shaken from the vines, our proposed solution used density separation to separate the two materials before the grapes reached the final collection bins.

The initial proposed solution was a sugar water or grape juice-based separation system. This approach was selected because it could be quick, effective, and relatively simple to fabricate using accessible materials. The design also considered several important constraints, including cost, fabrication time, available materials, potential loss of grape sugar content, and the short amount of time available between vine removal and collection.

## Functional Prototype

The Functional Prototype demonstrated a grape juice or sugar water-based density separator for removing spotted lanternflies from harvested grapes. The prototype was designed to show how density differences could be used to separate grapes and spotted lanternflies immediately after harvest.

In the proposed process, a mechanical shaker dislodges both grapes and spotted lanternflies from the vines. The mixture is then transported by conveyor belt toward the collection gondola. The density separator would be fastened on top of the gondola so that sorting could occur directly in the field without requiring major changes to the harvesting machine.

The prototype used a primary separation container filled with sugar water or grape juice. Grapes are expected to sink through the liquid and fall through an opening in the bottom of the container, while spotted lanternflies float to the top. A water-wheel-like mechanism then scoops the floating lanternflies into a separate container. After the grapes and liquid pass through the opening, they enter a second container with holes drilled into the bottom, allowing the grapes to be strained from the solution. The liquid is then pumped back into the initial container to create a controlled, recirculating stream.

During prototype testing, the team focused on motion, interference, and component connections. The wheel assembly was tested by mounting it to the separation container and rotating it through its full expected range of motion. Testing showed that the wheel could not fully rotate in the original tapered plastic bucket because it grinded against the container walls. As a result, the team determined that the final design should use a rectangular, untapered container to reduce wear, improve movement, and prevent unnecessary energy consumption.

The team also tested the stability of the water-wheel assembly, which included two plastic wheels, a shaft, and aluminum fins. During testing, the assembly slipped and fell apart easily, so the team determined that the components should be sealed together with epoxy to improve strength and environmental durability.

## Client Report

The Client Report presents the final proposed solution, explains how the prototype works, summarizes testing results, and recommends next steps for further development.

### Proposed Solution

The final proposed solution is a grape juice or sugar water-based density separation system that separates spotted lanternflies from grapes immediately after harvest. The design uses the density difference between grapes and spotted lanternflies: grapes sink in the solution, while spotted lanternflies float.

This design is intended to be installed directly on top of the gondola traveling next to the harvester. After grapes and spotted lanternflies are shaken from the vines, they enter the separator before reaching the final collection bins. This allows the separation process to occur in the field without requiring major changes to the harvester itself.

One important advantage of the design is that the liquid is recirculated using a pump. Because the sugar water or grape juice is reused throughout the system, harvesters would only need to be prepared with one batch of liquid before entering the field.

### How It Works / Is Used

The system works through a multi-step separation process:

1. A mechanical harvester shakes both grapes and spotted lanternflies from the vines.
2. The grape and lanternfly mixture is transported toward the collection gondola.
3. The mixture enters the density separator, which contains sugar water or grape juice.
4. Grapes sink and pass through an opening at the bottom of the primary container.
5. Spotted lanternflies float to the surface of the liquid.
6. A water-wheel-like mechanism scoops the floating spotted lanternflies into a separate container.
7. The grapes and liquid move into a second container with holes drilled into the bottom.
8. The grapes are strained from the liquid and sent to collection bins for processing.
9. The liquid is pumped back into the primary container to maintain a controlled stream.

The final design uses a primary container slanted at approximately 10 degrees to help direct grapes and liquid toward the bottom opening. The recirculating pump allows the system to run continuously during harvesting.

### Prototype and Testing Details

The prototype was constructed using two 3D printed plastic wheels, a 1-inch outer diameter hex bar shaft, and two aluminum fins cut from an aluminum perforated sheet.

The wheel components were designed with a hexagonal center cutout for the shaft and slots around the outer face for connecting the fins. The hex bar shaft was inserted through the center of the first wheel, the aluminum fins were placed into adjacent slots, and the second wheel was placed on the opposite side to complete the assembly.

The primary container was modified using a Dremel and hand saw. Semicircle shapes were cut into opposite sides of the bucket to mount the internal rod of the water wheel, and a rectangular opening was cut near the bottom corner to allow grapes and liquid to pass through.

Testing focused on whether the full assembly could rotate smoothly without grinding, clashing, sliding, or catching. The team found that the wheel could not fully rotate in the original bucket because the tapered sides interfered with the wheel motion. This showed that the final version should use a rectangular, untapered container.

The team also found that the water-wheel assembly was not stable enough when only fitted together mechanically. The components slipped and separated easily, so epoxy was recommended to seal the parts together and make the assembly stronger.

### Testing Results

The prototype testing showed that the overall design concept is promising, but several changes are needed before field implementation.

The main testing results were:

- The density separation concept is feasible because grapes and spotted lanternflies behave differently in liquid.
- The water-wheel mechanism can be used to remove floating lanternflies from the liquid surface.
- A tapered bucket is not appropriate for the final design because it causes wheel interference.
- A rectangular, untapered container should be used to allow smooth wheel rotation.
- The wheel components need to be sealed with epoxy to prevent slipping and improve durability.
- The pump system allows the liquid to be recirculated, reducing the amount of liquid needed during harvesting.

The final estimated bill of materials for the prototype was $183.34. This included the hex bar, PVC tubing, submersible pumps, plastic tote box, aluminum perforated sheet, 3D printed components, and epoxy.

### Client Feedback

Jennifer Russo confirmed that the idea was new and not something she or her colleagues had previously considered. She suggested several practical improvements, including using free-run juice instead of water to better protect grape quality, allowing leaves and other material other than grapes to be removed during the process, and placing the device directly on top of the gondola so that sorting could happen in the field without modifying the harvester.

She also noted that the device should be easy to remove when it is not needed. Overall, her feedback supported the design concept while also identifying clear improvements for future versions.

### Conclusion and Recommendation

Overall, the density separation system is promising and worth further development, but it requires more advanced testing before it can be considered field ready.

The team recommends continuing development of the design with several improvements. First, the sugar-to-water ratio should be adjusted to match the sugar content of specific grape varieties. This would reduce the risk of diluting grape sugar content during separation. Another option is to use grape slush or free-run juice instead of water, since this would more closely match the natural sugar concentration of the grapes.

Second, future prototypes should address pump reliability. Since grape juice or grape slush could become sticky or cause clogging, the design should use pumps with strong filtration systems or pumps that are easy to clean.

Third, more design work is needed to ensure that the separator can be adapted to different types of vineyard harvesters and gondolas. The system should be easy to install, remove, and clean.

Based on the prototype results and client feedback, the proposed density separator is structurally promising, water-retentive, and capable of running smoothly with efficient liquid recirculation. With additional testing and design refinement, this system could become a practical field solution for reducing spotted lanternfly contamination during grape harvesting.
